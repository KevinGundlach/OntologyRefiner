## THE  CHEMICAL COMPOSITION OF  THE PASSIVE FILM ON  Fe-24Cr AND Fe-24Cr-11Mo  STUDIED BY  AES,  XPS AND SIMS

## S.  MISCHLER, A. VOGEL,  H. J.  MATHIEU and D. LANDOLT

Materials Department, Swiss  Federal Institute of  Technology  (EPFL).  CH-1007 Lausanne, Switzerland

Abstraet--A systematic study of  the  composition and  of  the  thickness of  passive films formed  at  65°C  on Fe-24Cr  and  Fe-24Cr-11Mo model  alloys in  an  acid sulfate  lectrolyte inthe  presence and  absence of chloride ion  at  different potentials has  been  carried out. Non-destructive XPS, AES  depth  profiling and dynamic  SIMS were  applied to  the study of the average concentration and  the depth distribution of cations and  anions in  the films. The  apparent film thickness and  the average metal ion  concentration were  found  to be  little affected by  the  presence or  absence of chloride ion  in  the electrolyte. Molybdenum influenced the depth  distribution of  chloride ion  in  the  films.

## INTRODUCTION

STAINLESS  steels owe their corrosion resistance to  the presence of  a  thin surface oxide film. The characterization of  such  films, therefore, is an  important field of  corrosion science. Surface  analysis methods,  in  particular AES, XPS  and SIMS  have  been applied to  the study  of  the  chemical composition of  passive oxide  films on stainless steels for  a  number of  years and  results obtained by  different research groups  have been  reviewed in  the  literature. 1,2 It is generally accepted that passive oxide films on iron-chromium  alloys consist predominantly of  trivalent chromium oxide. 3 Their thickness is 2-3  nm typically and  depends  on  applied potential. 4  The  presence in  the films of  cations of  iron, of  molybdenum or  of  other alloying elements has  also been documented  depending on alloy composition. 5-7  Incorporation of  chloride ions  has been  observed by  some authors but  not  by  others.1

Molybdenum  is  frequently added to  ferritic and austenitic stainless steels to improve  their resistance to  pitting corrosion in  chloride media.  It is  not  known at present whether  passive films on molybdenum containing alloys formed  in  chloride media  have  the same  composition as  those formed  in  other media  and  how they differ from films  on similar alloys not  containing molybdenum. Such information could possibly be  of  use  for the  elucidation of  pit initiation mechanisms  on  stainless steels and  the  role of  molybdenum in  this.

Most of  the  published studies of  passive films  use  only  one surface analysis method  and  cover a  limited range  of  experimental conditions. Results, however,  are more easily interpreted when they  can  be  compared  on  a  relative basis.

Preferably, they  ought  to  be  substantiated by  different analysis techniques. For these  reasons, a study  was initiated in  our  laboratory with  the  aim of  providing a comprehensive set  of  experimental data  for  the  determination of  the  influence of applied potential, of  alloyed molybdenum and  of  the  presence of  chloride ion  in  the electrolyte on the composition  and thickness of the passive  films  formed on

anodically  polarized  high purity  iron-chromium  alloys  with and  without alloyed molybdenum. AES depth profiling, non-destructive XPS  and dynamic  SIMS  were employed  for  the  characterization of  t h e   films. The  average concentration  and the depth distribution of  m e t a l   c a t i o n s and of  electrolyte anions  in  the  film  and the  film thickness were determined.

## EXPERIMENTAL METHOD

The composition of  the  two alloys used  is given  in  Table  1.  The Fe-24Cr-11Mo alloy contained a higher molybdenum concentration than  the  usual ferritic stainless steels in  order to  facilitate analysis of this element in  the  passive film. The alloys which  are  single phase  were  heat  treated at  1300°C  for 58  h  to homogenize  the  elemental concentration. Grain  size after heat treatment was  in  the millimeter range. The alloys contained a  few  chromium oxide  inclusions.

The  electrodes were  machined  in the  form  of  rodsfrom which  rotating disk electrodes were  fabricated. For the  determination of  polarization curves  the  disk  electrode of  3.57  mm  diameter (electrode area 0.1  cm  2)  was embedded in  epoxy.  For  potentiostatic measurements  involving surface analysis identical electrodes were  used  but  without epoxy  insulation. By bringing the  disk  into contact with  the  electrolyte surface and  slightly pulling back,  a  meniscus was formed  which  prevented contact of  the  side of  the  rod with  the  electrolyte. The rotation rate was 3000 rpm in  all experiments.

Two  electrolytes were used,  0.1  M  H2SO 4  -I0.4  M  Na2SO 4  referred to  as  electrolyte A  and 0.1  M HeSO 4  + 0.4 M  Na2SO 4  + 0.12  M  NaCI referred to  as  electrolyte B.  All  experiments were  carried out  in de-aerated solutions under  argon.  The temperature was 65°C. This  temperature was chosen  because preliminary experiments had  shown that passive films formed  at  that temperature were  slightly thicker than those formed  at 25°C and  thus easier to  study. At  the  temperature of  the  experiments the Fe-Cr  alloy but  not  the  Fe-Cr-Mo alloy exhibited pitting in  the  electrolyte B.

Electrode pretreatment consisted of  mechanical  polishing with  SiC paper  and with  l~tm  alumina powder. The electrodes were washed with ethanol  and doubly distilled water  before  being  used. Polarization curves were  determined by  applying potential steps and  plotting the  current measured  after 400  s. The measurements  were  started at  a  cathodic potential of  -650  mV  (all potentials in  this paper  are given  with  respect to  the  normal  hydrogen  electrode). A  potentiostat (Solartron 1286)  controlled by a function generator (AMEL 568) was used.  Electrodes used for  surface analysis were mechanically polished and  polarized potentiostatically. Three  polarization conditions were  studied; the  condition (CA) involved cathodic polarization at  -650  mV  for 1  rain. The polarization condition (PL)  involved cathodic polarization at  -650  mV  for  1  min followed by anodic  polarization for  1  h  at  200  mV.  The polarization condition (PH)  was  the same  as  (PL)  but  anodic polarization was  at 500  mV. The  current was  monitored as a  function of  time  using an  HP  310  computer. At  the end  of  a  polarization experiment the  electrode was removed  from  the  electrolyte with  the  potential still applied, rinsed with  doubly  distilled water  and  dried with  argon. It was stored in  a  desiccator under  argon  for a  maximum  of  2  h  before being  introduced into the  surface analysis apparatus and  immediately analysed.

## DISCUSSION

The  characterization  of passive films by  AES, XPS and  SIMS is  s u b j e c t   t o  t h e limitations inherent in  all ex  situ methods,  t An important  consideration, therefore, is

to  what  extent results are  affected by  the  rinsing and  sample  transfer procedures used and what reproducibility can be achieved. The former  point  has  been discussed elsewhere. 15,18 Suffice here  to  mention  that SIMS  experiments performed  with  the 180 isotope indicate that  at  potentials (PH) the  post-oxidation of  passive films is negligible. For  the  potentials (PL)  such  experiments are  not  available but  it may be reasonably supposed  that the  behavior is  similar. The presented data  for  (PH) and (PL), therefore, are  believed to  be  representative ofthe  passive film. The (CA) data, on the  other hand,  represent the  film formed  in  air during  rinsing and transfer and their reproducibility is  limited. It  is  clear from a  comparison  of  the  results that the chemical composition of  the  two  types of  films differs  ubstantially.

Three  parameters were  varied in  the  present study: the  potential, alloyed Mo and the  presence of  C1-  in  the  electrolyte. The observed influence of  applied potential on film thickness in  the  passive region (Fig. 5)  and  on  the  Mo content of  the films (Fig. 6) is  in  qualitative agreement  with the  literature 2'7  although  observed  differences between  (PL)  and  (PH)  are slight. Alloyed Mo under  the  present conditions does  not affect the  thickness of  the  passive film  in  a  significant way (Fig. 5),  but  it alters its composition  because  of  incorporation of Mo  (Fig.  6).  The  growth-dissolution behavior of  the  passive films as  revealed by  the  measured  current-time transients (Fig. 3)  is apparently the  same  in  presence and  absence of  alloyed Mo. This  indicates that  at  the  potentials applied  in  this study  the  electrochemical properties of  the passive film are  not  significantly affected by  the  molybdenum. In  all experiments the applied  potentials were well  below the  critical pitting potential and no current instabilities of  the  type  associated in  the  literature with  metastable pitting 19  were observed in  this study.

The presence of  chloride ion  in  the  electrolyte had little effect on the  measured thickness of  the  passive films, although the  data of  Fig. 5  suggest that films formed  in electrolyte B are  slightly thinner. The electrolyte composition apparently affects the distribution of  molybdenum in  the  films; more molybdenum was found  in  the  outer part of  the  film formed  in  electrolyte A  than  in  electrolyte B as  is  evidenced by  the ratio profiles Mo/Fe + Cr + Mo  of  Fig.  10.

The XPS, AES  and SIMS  data  suggest that under  the  conditions of  the  present study  electrolyte anions  are  incorporated into the  passive films formed  on  Fe-24Cr and Fe-24Cr-llMo.  The presence of  sulfate is  deduced  from XPS  and AES, the presence of  Cl-  from  all three methods.  The  distribution fthese species in  the film is not  uniform,  however,  they  are  concentrated in  the  outer part  of  the  film.  The thickness of  the  chloride containing region of  the  passive films can  be  estimated from Fig.  15 taking the  50%  value  of  the  maximum amplitude  of  the  C1- profile as  a measure.  One obtains in  this way 0.5  nm (PL)  and  0.8  nm (PH),  respectively for the Fe-24Cr  alloy, 0.4  nm  (PL)  and  0.7  nm  (PH),  respectively, for  the  Fe-24Cr-llMo alloy. Since  the  overall film thickness at  a  given  potential is comparable  for the  two alloys it is concluded that Mo affects the  chloride ion  distribution i the  film relatively more than  the  overall film  thickness. A  similar effect of  Mo  on the  sulfur profile at potential (PH) appears in  Fig.  12.

Different interpretations of the  observed  concentration-depth distribution of chloride ion,  are a priori possible. Firstly, surface analysis methods such  as  AES, XPS and  SIMS yield average values of  concentration over  the  analysed area which  in the  present study is 200 x 200/~m for AES, 700 × 700/~m for SIMS and  3 x 3  mm  for

XPS. It cannot  be  excluded that on  a  smaller scale the  concentration is non-uniform.

For  example,  if the  films contain pores  or  defects of  nanometer  size, adsorption of electrolyte anions  at  the  pore  walls  could  possibly lead  to  the  observation of  a concentration profile over  a finite thickness. In  the  present case,  this is  unlikely, however,  because  one would expect  to  find  the  concentration maximum at  the surface rather than  at  some distance from  it (Figs 8  and  9). Another  possible way to interpret the  observed  chloride profiles is  by  postulating trapping of  anions in  a  gel like  outer  film  consisting of precipitated hydroxides.  Such  a  hypothesis is  in contradiction with  the  SIMS  data  of  Fig.  14,  however.  They show only  adsorbed hydroxyl ions  but  no  hydroxide film  of  a  thickness of  several atomic  layers.

A  third and perhaps  more plausible interpretation is  that electrolyte anions  are truly incorporated into  the  passive film  where they  can  occupy  positions of  oxygen ions. This  poses  no  conceptual difficulties in  the case  of  chloride ions, 2°'2t but  is more difficult to  visualize for sulfate. The structure of  the  passive films on  the  alloys used  is not known  but according  to McBee  and Kruger 22 the  films  are  expected  to  be amorphous.  An amorphous  structure is  expected to  facilitate the  accommodation  of electrolyte anions  but  the  problem  needs  further elucidation. If  electrolyte anions are  incorporated into the  film one  may ask  why they are  located predominantly in  the outer  part  and why  molybdenum  affects their depth-distribution. At present  no satisfactory explanation for  this behavior  is  available. According  to  Takahashi et al. ,23 who studied anodic films on  aluminium  in  a  phosphate electrolyte, the  extent of incorporation of  electrolyte anions in  the  outer part of  the  barrier film depends  on  the transference number of  the  metal  ions in  the  oxide. Passive films on Fe-Cr  and  FeCr-Mo  alloys are  believed to  be n-type  semi-conductors. 24'2s Another  approach, therefore, might  consider the  effect of  Mo  on  the  space  charge in  the  film. 26  Further studies using in situ experimental techniques will  be needed to elucidate these questions.

## CONCLUSIONS

Surface analysis data  obtained by AES, XPS  and SIMS  on passive films formed under well defined  electrochemical conditions in the presence  and absence of chloride ions  on two ferritic iron-chromium  alloys with  and without  molybdenum lead to the following conclusions which are strictly valid  for  the experimental conditions of  this study.

- (1)  Alloyed  molybdenum has  only  a  minor  effect on  the  thickness of  the  passive film  and  on  its growth-dissolution kinetics.
- (2)  The presence or  absence  of  chloride ion  in  the  electrolyte does  not  significantly change  the  average metal  ion  concentration in  the  film and has  only  a  minor effect on the  film  thickness.
- (3)  Molybdenum  and  chloride ions  rather than  being  distributed uniformly in  the film  are  concentrated in  its outer part.
- (4)  Alloyed  molybdenum influences the  chloride distribution in  the  film.
