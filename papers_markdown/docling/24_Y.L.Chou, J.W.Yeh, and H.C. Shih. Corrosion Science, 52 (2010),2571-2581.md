## 1. Introduction

In the past, the development of conventional alloy systems has been based primarily on one principal element, such as iron-, aluminium-, magnesium-, and nickel-based alloys, limiting the number of applicable alloy systems, even though a substantial amount of other elements is incorporated for property/processing enhancement [1,2]. In the 1990s, Inoue et al. [3] obtained the highly glass forming alloy by using at least three different elements with significantly different atomic radii. In addition, Greer [4] came up with the ''confusion principle' in which he stated that the possibility of crystallisation could be lowered if the number of alloying elements was large. In the last two decades, a wide range of multicomponent amorphous alloys, including titanium-, nickel-, zirconium-, iron-, cobalt-, and copper-based alloy [5-10] systems have been developed. Multiple studies have been widely investigated the corrosion and passivation behaviour of these multicomponent amorphous alloys in a variety of electrolytes [11-34]. However, the design concept of afore-mentioned multicomponent amorphous alloys was, once again, based on one principal element. In an attempt to break through the cognitive realm of alloy design, a new multicomponent amorphous alloy system was subsequently developed [35-39]. This new alloy system is also called ''high-entropy alloys' which are composed of multiprincipal elements in equimolar or near-equimolar ratios. Each element in the alloy sys-

Therefore, the purpose of this study is to investigate the electrochemical behaviour of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys under different environmental conditions.

## 2. Experimental

## 2.1. Test materials

Elements cobalt (Co), chromium (Cr), titanium (Ti), iron (Fe), and nickel (Ni) with purities of over 99 wt.% were present as granules as raw materials. The 39.5 wt.% Fe-60.5 wt.% Mo master alloy was used with the added molybdenum (Mo). This high-entropy alloy is a six-component alloy in the form of Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x , with 1.5 mol of Co, Ni, 0.5 mol of Ti, and 1 mol of the other two alloying elements and between zero and 1 mol of the added Mo. Table 1 presents the chemical compositions of the alloys in weight percent (wt.%). The multiprincipal-element Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloy system with various Mo contents with x from 0 to 1.0 was prepared in this work. An induction furnace was used to melt the high-entropy alloys at 1600 ° C after purging with argon. First, raw materials of Co, Fe, and Ni were placed in the crucible. When Co, Fe, and Ni had melted in liquid state, then Ti, Cr, and FeMo were added to the crucible in order according to their melting points. Finally, the melt was poured into a shell mold preheated at 1100 ° C. The size of the ingots was 8 -7 -14 (cm). The high-entropy alloy cylinder for electrochemical measurements was obtained by cutting the bulk material by electric arc line into pieces that were 3 mm thick and 8 mm in diameter. Each test specimen was then cold mounted, using an epoxy resin to give an exposed surface area of 0.5 cm 2 for the high-entropy alloys. Before each electrochemical experiment, all specimens were mechanically ground using a series of 240-600 silicon carbide (SiC) grit papers and cleaned with acetone and distilled water.

## 2.3. Electrochemical measurements

The corrosion resistance of Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys was conducted in three different environmental conditions; 1 molar concentration (M) sodium hydroxide (NaOH), 0.5 M sulfuric acid (H2SO4), and 1 M sodium chloride (NaCl), which will be referred to as base, acid, and salt solution, respectively henceforth at 25 ° C under atmospheric pressure. The test solution was deaerated by bubbling purified nitrogen gas before and throughout the electrochemical tests to eliminate the effect of dissolved oxygen. The electrochemical polarisation was performed in a typical three electrode cell that consisted of a specimen as the working electrode, a silver/silver chloride (Ag/AgCl) in 3 M potassium chloride (KCl) as the reference electrode, and a platinum (Pt) sheet as the counter electrode. The Ag/AgCl scale (3 M KCl) is 0.208 V lower than the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) [49]:

Unless otherwise stated in this paper, all potentials recorded were referred to standard hydrogen electrode.

Potentiodynamic polarisation measurements were made at a scan rate of 1 mV/s from an initial potential of   0.5 V to a final potential of 1.5 V versus the open current potential (OCP). The potential was controlled and the current was measured using a potentiostat (Autolab PGSTAT30). A cyclic polarisation technique started at   0.5 V by scanning at 1 mV/s towards more positive potentials up to 1.5 V versus the OCP, at which value the potential scan was reversed and returned to where the polarisation began. Before either of these tests was conducted, the OCP was recorded for 15 min to yield a steady-state potential. Then, the specimen was cathodically polarised to a potential of   192 mVSHE for 5 min to reduce the existing surface films. Following electrochemical testing, multiple electrochemical parameters were determined: E corr , corrosion potential, i.e., the potential where zero current density [21]; E pp, primary passivation potential, the potential at which the maximum anodic current density reached in the active region [50]; E sp , secondary passivation potential, the potential at which the maximum anodic current density reached in the passive region [51]; E b, the potential at which the current density suddenly increases is determined to be the breakdown potential [20]; D E , the difference between E b and E pp is the estimated passive region width [42]; E prot , the protection potential where the forward and reverse scan crosses in the passive region [51]; E rp , the repassivation potential is considered to be the potential at which the cyclic polarisation curves are approximately horizontal on the potential reverse scan [42]; i cri , critical current density, the maximum anodic current density in the active region [50]; i pass , anodic current density values remain completely constant in the passive range [50]; the corrosion current density, i corr , is obtained from the curves using the intersection of the cathodic linear extrapolation (approximately   50 to   100 mV from the corrosion potential) and the corrosion potential horizontal line.

## 2.4. Surface morphology and chemical analysis

Following the polarisation experiment, the specimen was cleaned using distilled water and then dried in nitrogen. Immediately thereafter, the morphology and chemical composition of the corroded surface of the high-entropy alloy was investigated using a JEOL 5410 SEM.

## 3.2. Potentiodynamic polarisation curves

Fig. 3 plots the potentiodynamic polarisation curves of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys with various Mo contents ( x = 0, 0.1, 0.5, and 0.8) in 0.5 M H2SO4 solution. The potentiodynamic polarisation curve exhibited active-passive corrosion behaviour, and the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys yielded a wide passive region ( E b   E pp = D E &gt; 1100 mV), which indicated a tendency of the alloys to passivate. Moreover, the passive region of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5 Ti0.5Mo0 alloy was broader than that of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.1, Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.5, and Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.8 alloys. The E sp occurred for the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.8 alloy under 0.5 M H2SO4 solution, which may be attributed to the formation and dissolution of the molybdenum oxide film. Table 4 presents the electrochemical parameters of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x ( x = 0, 0.1, 0.5, and 0.8) in deaerated 0.5 M H2SO4 solution. The corrosion potential ( E corr ) of the Mo-containing alloys was more apparently more noble than that of the Mo-free alloy. However, the corrosion current densities ( i corr ) of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.1, Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.5, and Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.8 alloys were 78, 72, and 69 l A/cm 2 , respectively; the i corr value dropped to 30 l A/cm 2 when Mo was eliminated from the alloy, as in Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0. Furthermore, the passive current was increased in those alloys with Mo content. The primary passivation potential ( E pp) of the Mo-free alloy was lower than that of the Mo-containing alloys. The critical current density ( i cri) of the Mo-free alloy was a little higher than that of the Mo-containing alloys. Hence, the Mo alloying element addition has negative effect on the corrosion resistance of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys.

The broadest passive region and lowest passive current density under 0.5 M H2SO4 solution (Table 4) were registered in the Mofree alloy whose main characteristic is the highest presence of Cr [58]. Although the highest Mo content of Co1.5CrFeNi0.5Ti1.5Mo0.8 alloy, lower Cr content with respect to Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0 alloy, generated a higher passive current density along the passive region. In the passive region, the measured current comes from the dissolution of the oxide film. The dissolution current of the oxide film increases as the Cr content in the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x ( x = 0, 0.1, 0.5, and 0.8) alloys decreases, indicating that the addition of Cr can improve the stability of the oxide film. The addition of Mo is widely recognised for its beneficial effect on the corrosion resistance of the stainless steels and has been studied extensively in the literature [60-65]. Sugimoto and Sawada [60] concluded that Mo addition to austenitic stainless steels allows formation of a passive film consisted of a Cr (III) oxyhydroxide containing Mo 6+ ions, which reduces the corrosion current density in the active region for acidic solution. The corrosion current density in the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.1 alloy is lower than that in the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0 alloy, which may be attributed to the simultaneous presence of lower Cr and slightly higher Mo content (1.8 atom%) in the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.1 alloy (Table 2). As the Mo content increases from 0.5 to 0.8 mol in the high-entropy alloys, the interdendrite phase of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys is a Cr and Mo rich r phase, and the dendrite is a Cr and Mo depleted phase. The r phase is probably the most studied and undesirable phase in a stainless steel. Formation of r phase can occur in the austenitic, ferritic, and duplex stainless steels. Especially in duplex stainless steels, the formation of r phase is accelerated by increased amount of Cr and Mo [71,72]. The formation of that phase not only causes losses in ductility and toughness of the steel, but also reduces its corrosion resistance by removing Cr and Mo from the austenitic matrix [73]. Conejero et al. [57] studied premature corrosion failure of a 316L stainless steel plate exposed to a H2SO4 environment. It was reported that a 316L stainless steel plate has failed due to the presence of r phase, which is deleterious to corrosion performance of the stainless steels in H2SO4 solution. Therefore, the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x ( x = 0.5, 0.8) alloys possessed the lower corrosion current density in comparison with the Mo-free alloy in this study, which could be also attributed to the presence of r phase.

The hysteresis revealed by the cyclic polarisation curve provides information on the pitting corrosion rate and how readily a passive film repairs itself. Negative hysteresis occurs when the reverse scan current density is less than that for the forward scan, and positive hysteresis occurs when the reverse scan current density exceeds the forward scan current density [75].

Cyclic polarisation measurements were made at a scanning rate of 1 mV/s. The potential scan began at   0.5 V and continued in the anodic direction until the potential was 1.5 V versus the OCP, at which value the potential scan was reversed and returned to where the polarisation began. Fig. 5 plots the cyclic polarisation curves of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys with various Mo contents ( x = 0, 0.1, 0.5, and 0.8). The black and gray arrows next to the forward and reverse anodic branches indicate the potential scan directions. In terms of the electrochemical parameters shown in Table 6, the addition of Mo moves the E b in the noble direction. The cyclic polarisation curve of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0 alloy was referred to as a pseudo-passive curve because the curve did not exhibit a distinct E pp, but the E b was observed. The difference between E b and E pp was the estimated passive region width D E . The D E for the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0 alloy was 557 mV. As the Mo content in the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys increased from 0.1 to 0.8 mol, the D E varied between 1371 and 1429 mV. Therefore, the D E of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys broadened due to the effect of Mo, which is in agreement with Sedriks et al. [58]. The Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys ( x = 0.1, 0.5, and 0.8) alloys showed negative hysteresis in the cyclic polarisation curve, indicating that the Mo-containing alloys were resistant to pitting corrosion in 1 M NaCl solution, as can be seen in Fig. 5b-d. However, the positive hysteresis of the cyclic polarisation curve demonstrates that pitting of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0 alloy can be induced in 1 M NaCl solution, as in Fig. 5(a). The area contained within a positive hysteresis loop is related to the amount of pit propagation that occurs during the cycle, according to Wilde [76]. The intersection at the passive region defined protection potential ( E prot), below which the established pits on metal surface apparently could not keep on growing. In contrast, new pits would initiate only above E b. Between E prot and E b, new pits could not initiate, but old ones still could grow. Furthermore, the i corr of the Mo-containing alloys was lower than that of the Mo-free alloy. Therefore, adding Mo element has positive effect on the corrosion resistance of Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys in 1 M NaCl solution.

It has been well established that the addition of Mo to stainless steels improves the pitting corrosion resistance in media containing chloride [50,58,77-78]. It has been considered that Mo could form MoO 2   4 ions in the solid state in the outer regions of the film in neutral NaCl solutions according to Pourbaix diagrams [68]. This MoO 2   4 layer is cation selective and can serve as a very effective pitting inhibitor, which resists the incorporation of anions such as Cl   and Br   , allowing for the growth of the Cr oxide inner barrier layer. The resulting bipolar layer stabilises the oxide phase [65].

A passive film breaks down when the applied potential is raised into the transpassive region of the potentiodynamic polarisation curve, and the formation of pits can be initiated at discrete locations on the metal surface [85]. ## 5. Conclusions

The electrochemical characteristics of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x high-entropy alloys in solutions of 0.5 M H2SO4, 1 M NaCl, and NaOH have been studied in this paper. The potentiodynamic polarisation curves of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo x alloys in acidic solution exhibit active-passive corrosion behaviour, yielding an extensive passive region. The corrosion current density and passive current density of the Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5 alloy are significantly lower than those of the Co1.5CrFeNi0.5Ti1.5Mo0.1, Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.5, and Co1.5CrFeNi1.5Ti0.5Mo0.8 alloys. Therefore, the Mo-free alloy is more resistant to general corrosion than that of the Mo-containing alloys in acidic environments. On the other hand, the cyclic polarisation curves indicate that the Mo-free alloy tends to pitting corrosion in 1 M NaCl solution accounting for the lower breakdown potential and narrower range of potentials in the passive region.
