**Instructions**

Extract data hierarchically: Base Materials contain Conditioned Materials, which contain Experiments.

### Critic Agent Prompt

**Role and Objective**
You are a Schema Optimization Critic for a materials science data pipeline. Your goal is to iteratively improve a data extraction template by identifying relevant variables mentioned in a research paper that are currently missing from the template.

**Inputs Provided**
1. The source research paper (PDF).
2. The current Markdown Extraction Template.

**Instructions**
Analyze the source paper to identify experimental variables, environmental conditions, or material characteristics relevant to predicting corrosion pitting potential that are NOT currently requested in the Markdown template.

Output a comprehensively updated Markdown Extraction Template.
Add new variables in the appropriate hierarchical section (Base Material, Conditioned Material, or Experiment).
Never delete or remove an existing variable from the template, as it may be required for other papers in the corpus.

**Output Format**
Provide a brief summary of the changes made (e.g., added "surface finish" to Conditioned Material).
Provide the complete, updated Markdown template.

### Critical Shortcomings and Pitfalls

While this dual-agent refinement loop is a strong starting point, there are several significant hurdles you will face when deploying this over a 100-paper corpus.

**The "Estimation from Plots" Problem**
Instructing an LLM to estimate values from complex scientific plots is highly error-prone. [cite_start]In the provided Carroll & Lynskey paper, estimating precise pitting potentials from the potentiodynamic polarization curves with logarithmic current scales (like Figure 3) would be essentially impossible to do accurately without the accompanying text[cite: 2724, 2725]. Vision models struggle to align axes, differentiate overlapping lines of different alloys, and interpolate logarithmic scales. Relying on plot estimation will introduce severe hallucinations into your dataset.

**Schema Bloat and Template Degradation**
Your rule instructing the Critic to "never delete variables" will cause massive template bloat. After processing 20 papers, your template will contain dozens of highly specific, edge-case variables (e.g., "Teflon gasket torque," "argon gas purge time"). An overly massive prompt dilutes the Data Extractor's attention, increasing latency, driving up token costs, and degrading extraction accuracy for the core variables. 

**Nomenclature Inconsistencies**
Different authors use vastly different terminology for the same concepts. [cite_start]The Pessall & Nurminen paper refers to the scratch pitting potential as $E_c^{scr}$[cite: 2091]. [cite_start]The Malik et al. paper refers to pitting potential as $E_{pit}$[cite: 1667]. The Extractor must be able to harmonize these terms, or the Critic will erroneously create duplicate variables in the template (e.g., one bullet for "breakdown potential" and another for "pitting potential").

**Rigid Hierarchical Mapping**
The strict "Base -> Conditioned -> Experiment" hierarchy will not fit every experimental design. Some papers test a single base material under 50 different environmental conditions. [cite_start]Other papers, like the Malik et al. study, test dozens of different base materials (conventional vs. high alloy steels) in a single, identical seawater experiment[cite: 1569]. Forcing the Extractor to rewrite the same "Experiment" data under 15 different "Conditioned Material" headings will cause redundant outputs and context window exhaustion.
